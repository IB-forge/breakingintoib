Investment Banking Technicals

Discounted Cash Flow (DCF)

DCF is a valuation method used to estimate the value of an investment based on its expected future cash flows. It involves projecting these cash flows and discounting them back to their present value using a discount rate that reflects the risk of the investment.

In interviews, DCF is crucial as it demonstrates your understanding of fundamental valuation principles. You'll likely be asked to explain the components of a DCF model, such as projecting free cash flows, determining the discount rate (WACC), and calculating terminal value.

Example: Valuing a company's stock by projecting its free cash flows for the next five years, discounting them using the company's WACC, and adding the present value of the terminal value (calculated using a perpetuity growth method).

Comparable Company Analysis (Comps)

Comps involves valuing a company by comparing it to similar companies (peers) that are publicly traded. Key financial metrics and ratios (e.g., EV/EBITDA, P/E) are used to derive an implied valuation range for the target company.

Interviews often assess your ability to select appropriate peer companies, calculate relevant valuation multiples, and justify your assumptions. You might be asked to walk through a comps analysis for a hypothetical company.

Example: Valuing a software company by comparing its EV/Revenue and P/E ratios to those of its publicly traded competitors, adjusting for differences in growth rates, profitability, and risk profiles.

Leveraged Buyout (LBO)

LBO is a financial transaction where a company is acquired using a significant amount of borrowed money (debt). The acquirer (typically a private equity firm) uses the target company's assets and cash flows to service the debt over time.

In interviews, LBO modeling is a common topic. You'll need to understand the mechanics of an LBO, including sources and uses of funds, debt repayment schedules, and how returns are generated for the private equity sponsor.

Example: A private equity firm acquires a stable, cash-generating business using a mix of debt and equity. The firm aims to improve the company's operations, repay the debt, and sell the company at a higher valuation after a few years.

Precedent Transactions

Precedent Transactions involves valuing a company based on the prices paid for similar companies in past acquisitions. It helps determine a reasonable valuation range by analyzing transaction multiples (e.g., EV/EBITDA, EV/Revenue) from comparable deals.

Interviews often require you to discuss how to select relevant precedent transactions, adjust for differences between deals, and interpret the resulting valuation range. You might be asked to analyze a set of transactions and justify your conclusions.

Example: Valuing a food and beverage company by examining recent acquisitions of similar-sized companies in the same industry, considering factors like deal structure, synergies, and market conditions.
